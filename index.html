Drug Class Onset Duration Metabolism / Notes Max dose (per appointment)
Lidocaine Amide 3–5 min Moderate (prolonged with epinephrine)
  • Metabolized in liver by microsomal CYP‑450
  • Active metabolites M.E.G.X. and G.X.
  • Vasodilator (use with epinephrine), so risk of bleeding
  • 2% lidocaine used with epinephrine as a vasoconstrictor
 500 mg
Mepivacaine Amide 3–5 min Short (~20 min without vasoconstrictor)
  • Metabolized in liver by CYP‑450 (like lidocaine)
  • Slight intrinsic vasoconstriction — may be used without epinephrine
400 mg
Prilocaine Hydrochloride (Citanest®) Amide 3–5 min 10 min (no vasoconstrictor) → up to ~90 min (with VC)
  • Metabolized in liver and kidneys via amidases
  • Produces ortho‑toluidine (risk of methemoglobinemia, esp. G6PD)
  • Hepatic and renal dysfunction can alter its kinetics
    • Precautions:
    • Metabolized in both liver and kidney and small degree in lungs yielding various metabolites
    • Ortho-toluidine may cause methemoglobinemia (especially in G6PD deficiency)
600 mg
Articaine (Septocaine®) Amide (contains ester group) 1–6 min
  • Moderate
  • Rapid clearance (t½ ≈ 20 min)
  • Metabolized mainly in blood by plasma carboxyesterase and to lesser extent in liver (CYP) — suitable in renal dysfunction
  • Contains both ester & amide moieties
  • Suitable for patients with renal dysfunction
 No absolute max (limited by epinephrine)
Bupivacaine (Marcaine®) Amide 2–10 min Long (≈ 90–180 min)
  • Metabolized in liver by amidases to inactive metabolites
  • High vasodilator activity (often paired with epinephrine)
90 mg

Lecture 4: Management of Psychiatric Disorders
Drug Class / Group Indication MOA Adverse effects / Interactions
Levodopa–Carbidopa (Sinemet®) Anti‑Parkinson (dopamine precursor) Parkinson disease
  • Levodopa converted to dopamine in CNS
  • Carbidopa inhibits peripheral decarboxylase to increase CNS availability
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Hallucinations
  • Dyskinesia
  • Xerostomia
  • Taste alterations
Benztropine (Cogentin®) Muscarinic antagonist (antimuscarinic) Adjunct for tremor & rigidity in PD
  • Blocks CNS muscarinic receptors
  • Reduces cholinergic overactivity
  • Antimuscarinic effects
  • Xerostomia (important in dentistry)
Entacapone COMT inhibitor Adjunct to levodopa in PD
  • Inhibits COMT
  • Reduces peripheral L‑dopa metabolism
  • Abnormal taste
  • Orthostatic hypotension
Selegiline MAO‑B inhibitor Adjunct in PD to prolong DA action
  • Inhibits MAO‑B
  • Decreases dopamine metabolism in CNS
  • Xerostomia
Chlorpromazine / Fluphenazine / Haloperidol First‑generation (typical) antipsychotics
  • Psychosis
  • Acute mania
  • Tourette syndrome
  • D2 receptor antagonists (mesolimbic)
  • Reduce psychotic symptoms
  • High risk extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS)
  • Tardive dyskinesia (especially haloperidol)
  • Anticholinergic/autonomic effects
  • Xerostomia
  • Orthostatic hypotension
Clozapine Second‑generation (atypical) antipsychotic Refractory schizophrenia
  • Blocks 5‑HT2 and D receptors
  • Also blocks muscarinic receptors
  • Also blocks histaminic receptors
  • Also blocks α‑adrenergic receptors
  • Agranulocytosis (requires CBC monitoring)
  • Metabolic effects
  • Sedation
  • Xerostomia
Risperidone Second‑generation antipsychotic
  • Schizophrenia
  • Bipolar mania
  • Blocks 5‑HT2 receptors more than D2 receptors
  • Metabolic side effects
  • Sedation
  • Autonomic effects including xerostomia
Donepezil Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor Dementia due to Alzheimer disease
  • Reversibly inhibits centrally active acetylcholinesterase
  • Increases acetylcholine
  • Nausea
  • Diarrhea
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Bradycardia
  • Hypotension
Rivastigmine Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor
  • Dementia due to Alzheimer disease
  • Parkinson disease–associated dementia
  • Reversibly inhibits acetylcholinesterase
  • Increases acetylcholine
  • Gastrointestinal effects similar to donepezil
  • Bradycardia
  • Hypotension
Memantine NMDA receptor antagonist Moderate–severe Alzheimer disease (adjunct)
  • Blocks NMDA receptors
  • Limits calcium influx
  • Reduces excitotoxicity
  • Dizziness
  • Other CNS effects
Aducanumab (Aduhelm®) Amyloid‑directed monoclonal antibody (IV) Alzheimer disease (targets amyloid plaques)
  • Reduces amyloid β plaques
  • Antibody‑mediated clearance
  • IV therapy risks
  • Disease‑modifying intent (requires specific clinical guidance)
Riluzole (Exservan®) ALS agent (glutamate modulator) Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis
  • Inhibits glutamate release
  • Inactivates sodium channels
  • Nausea
  • Oral hypoesthesia
Amitriptyline / Clomipramine Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs)
  • Major depression
  • OCD (clomipramine)
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Inhibit reuptake of norepinephrine (NE) via NET
  • Inhibit reuptake of serotonin (5‑HT) via SERT
  • Block α‑adrenergic receptors
  • Block histaminic receptors
  • Block muscarinic receptors
  • Antimuscarinic effects (including xerostomia)
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Sedation
  • Tachycardia
  • Interacts with benzodiazepines
  • Potentiates epinephrine in local anesthesia
Phenelzine Monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI) Refractory depression
  • Inactivates monoamine oxidase enzymes
  • Increases monoamine concentrations
  • Drowsiness
  • Orthostatic hypotension
  • Xerostomia
  • Constipation
  • Dangerous tyramine food interactions → hypertensive crisis
  • Potentiates sympathomimetics (monitor epinephrine use)
Fluoxetine / Sertraline / Citalopram / Escitalopram Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
  • First‑line for major depression
  • PTSD
  • OCD
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Panic disorder
  • Orofacial pain
  • Selective inhibition of SERT
  • Increased synaptic serotonin
  • GI irritation
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Weight changes
  • Increased suicidal thoughts in younger patients
  • Bruxism
  • Risk of serotonin syndrome
  • Drug interactions with NSAIDs (increased GI bleed risk)
  • Fluoxetine and paroxetine inhibit CYP2D6
Venlafaxine / Duloxetine Serotonin‑norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs)
  • Depression
  • Duloxetine also used for neuropathic pain
  • Inhibit reuptake of serotonin
  • Inhibit reuptake of norepinephrine
  • Similar to SSRIs (GI effects)
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Bruxism
  • Increased blood pressure
  • Increased heart rate
  • Insomnia
  • Anxiety
  • Xerostomia
Ketamine / Esketamine Rapid‑acting antidepressant (NMDA antagonist) Treatment‑resistant depression
  • Enhances glutamate release
  • Blocks NMDA receptors
  • Increases BDNF
  • Enhances neuroplasticity
  • Increased blood pressure
  • Potential tolerance and dependence
  • Dizziness
  • Sialorrhea
  • Altered taste
Trazodone (‑zodone) Serotonin receptor antagonist (5‑HT2a antagonist)
  • Depression
  • Often used for sedative properties
  • Blocks selective serotonin receptors (5‑HT2a)
  • Minor effects on other receptors
  • Sedation
  • Xerostomia
Bupropion Dopamine‑norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor
  • Depression
  • Tobacco cessation
  • Increases synaptic dopamine by inhibiting reuptake
  • Increases synaptic norepinephrine by inhibiting reuptake
  • Lower risk of sexual dysfunction
  • Seizure risk at high doses
  • Xerostomia (possible)
St. John's Wort (hyperforin) Herbal antidepressant (OTC)
  • Mild–moderate depression
  • Mood swings
  • Active ingredient hyperforin
  • Multiple actions
  • Enzyme induction
  • Induces CYP3A4
  • Induces CYP2C9
  • Many drug interactions
  • May potentiate MAOIs
  • May potentiate SSRIs
  • Increased risk of serotonin syndrome
Lithium carbonate Mood stabilizer
  • Bipolar disorder (mania)
  • Bipolar depression maintenance
  • Exact mechanism unknown
  • Modulates neurotransmitter release
  • Modulates intracellular signaling
  • Narrow therapeutic index
  • Xerostomia
  • Metallic taste
  • Sedation
  • Polyuria and polydipsia (nephrogenic diabetes insipidus)
  • Tremor
  • NSAIDs increase lithium levels
  • Some antibiotics increase lithium levels
  • Renal excretion
Valproic acid Anticonvulsant (used in bipolar)
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Seizures
  • Multiple anticonvulsant mechanisms
  • Potentiates GABA
  • Teratogen (spina bifida)
  • Thrombocytopenia (bleeding risk)
Carbamazepine Anticonvulsant (used in bipolar)
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Seizures
  • Blocks voltage‑gated sodium channels
  • Provides mood stabilization effects
  • Teratogen (cleft palate)
  • Bone marrow suppression
