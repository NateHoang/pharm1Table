Amide Local Anesthetics
Drug Class Onset Duration Metabolism / Notes Max dose (per appointment)
Lidocaine Amide 3–5 min Moderate (prolonged with epinephrine)
  • Metabolized in liver by microsomal CYP‑450
  • Active metabolites M.E.G.X. and G.X.
  • Vasodilator (use with epinephrine), so risk of bleeding
  • 2% lidocaine used with epinephrine as a vasoconstrictor
 500 mg
Mepivacaine Amide 3–5 min Short (~20 min without vasoconstrictor)
  • Metabolized in liver by CYP‑450 (like lidocaine)
  • Slight intrinsic vasoconstriction — may be used without epinephrine
400 mg
Prilocaine Hydrochloride (Citanest®) Amide 3–5 min 10 min (no vasoconstrictor) → up to ~90 min (with VC)
  • Metabolized in liver and kidneys via amidases
  • Produces ortho‑toluidine (risk of methemoglobinemia, esp. G6PD)
  • Hepatic and renal dysfunction can alter its kinetics
    • Precautions:
    • Metabolized in both liver and kidney and small degree in lungs yielding various metabolites
    • Ortho-toluidine may cause methemoglobinemia (especially in G6PD deficiency)
600 mg
Articaine (Septocaine®) Amide (contains ester group) 1–6 min
  • Moderate
  • Rapid clearance (t½ ≈ 20 min)
  • Metabolized mainly in blood by plasma carboxyesterase and to lesser extent in liver (CYP) — suitable in renal dysfunction
  • Contains both ester & amide moieties
  • Suitable for patients with renal dysfunction
 No absolute max (limited by epinephrine)
Bupivacaine (Marcaine®) Amide 2–10 min Long (≈ 90–180 min)
  • Metabolized in liver by amidases to inactive metabolites
  • High vasodilator activity (often paired with epinephrine)
90 mg
